Destiny and Will (R05)

Dario Ricardo De Santiago Sasinka Affiliation: https://www.frc.utn.edu.ar ORCID: 0000-0002-7684-9532 February 2026

Abstract

The text argues that time is not a container of events but a virtual dimension of transformation, so that only the present exists ontologically, and it is updated irreversibly. Reality is organized into three levels: a microscopic level of indeterminacy, a hyperscopic level dominated by the inertia of global trajectories, and a mesoscopic level where will emerges as the capacity to orient possibilities without breaking the world's framework. In this scheme, chance distributes probabilities, destiny acts as structural persistence, and free will consists of traversing a contour without a script, being compatible with a universe devoid of teleological purpose.

1. Warning!

This text is written to be thought about. It is philosophy: ontology, logic, and epistemology. It attempts to explain the universe we observe and for which time, chance, will, and destiny within it would have to make sense.

2. The dilemma of time

Time is often imagined as a place: a line, an axis, an extra dimension through which we advance. Mathematically, this representation is correct and useful for modeling, but ontologically it is misleading.¹

Time is a virtual dimension: a parameter that measures transformation, not a continent that harbors realities. If it were a real dimension like spatial ones, all realities — past, present, future — would exist simultaneously. But that is not what we experience.²

Time is not a space where something happens, but the rhythm in which reality transforms itself. This transformation is observable because there is no universal coordinate of time; rather, it is a relative characteristic of each entity. We do not travel through time: we are transformed by it.

The past does not exist. It is not stored anywhere nor waiting to be retrieved. It was consumed to build the present. The future does not exist as something already given. It does not wait, it is not written, it does not await us. It is merely the open consequence of what happens now.

Ontologically, only the present exists. Reality does not accumulate or archive itself: it actualizes, it overwrites what already existed, reusing its parts, being a conservative system in its transformation. Each instant replaces the previous one and erases it. The universe does not keep backups. To exist is to continuously overwrite reality.

It is important to clarify what this overwriting means. It is not destruction nor re-creation from nothing: it is transformation. The constituents — matter and energy — persist and move; what ceases to exist is the previous configuration, because its elements are no longer in that arrangement. It is analogous to a chess game: pieces are neither destroyed nor created. Between two consecutive instants, most pieces remain exactly where they were; only those that were moved change position. The previous configuration ceases to exist not because it was destroyed, but because some pieces are no longer where they were. The past does not disappear: it becomes the present with the minimum necessary change.

However, this present is not homogeneous. The most persistent error is to believe that all of reality is made of the same kind of things, governed by the same kind of necessity. That is not the case. The universe is stratified.

The following table illustrates the difference between conceiving time as a static container and as a perspectival actualization process:

3. The three levels of reality

3.1 The microscopic level

At the deepest level, the microscopic, there is neither will nor decision. Nor is there chaos. There is indeterminacy: a basic ontological openness. Before any definition, multiple possible paths exist. This level does not choose, but it allows something new to occur.

Its ontological function is precise: to eliminate machine determinism, i.e., the fixed script. Without this openness, the system would simply be an automaton executing a pre-established causal chain, and you would be reading this text because you were destined to do so, not because you chose to read it.

Will is not born here. But without this level, will would be impossible.

3.2 The hyperscopic level

At the opposite extreme lies the hyperscopic: the domain of cosmological order. There reside the regularities that persist, the robust and continuous trajectories that give stability to the whole.

This level behaves deterministically not because its microscopic constituents are individually deterministic, but because the system is so vast that individual indeterminacies average out and cancel each other. The aggregate moves as an inertial whole where local properties are absorbed by the statistical behavior of the ensemble.

This level is not sensitive to local choices. Not because intervention is forbidden, but because the scale itself renders it immune. Will does not reach here, not due to lack of free will, but because of its limited range of action.

3.3 The mesoscopic level

Between both extremes opens the only territory where free will can have meaning: the mesoscopic. The human, biological, and historical realm. The place where a minimal difference can be amplified following rules that lead to a cascade of actions, a decision, a life.

Will operates here. Not as a mysterious force nor as a special substance, but as the capacity of complex systems to organize indeterminacy and turn it into direction. Will does not create new possibilities. It does not break the framework. It does something more subtle: it tips the balance. It decides which way to go within a domain it did not choose.

Will is not a phenomenon of direct microscopic indeterminacy, as if a decision were the result of a random collapse. Microscopic indeterminacy makes the system non-deterministic, but it does not direct it.⁸ The direction comes from the complex architecture that operates on that openness: a neural network is neither random nor deterministic; it is a system that captures the basal openness and turns it into a meaningful trajectory through its internal states, memory, and objectives. Just as a language model does not always repeat the same response to the same input, without being for that reason unpredictable or chaotic.

This conception eliminates the need for a "homunculus": a magical or little driver inside the head that would make decisions.⁹ Will is an emergent property of the whole system, not of an internal agent.

4. Chance, destiny, and will

Here a persistent confusion often appears: identifying chance with chaos. Chance is not absence of structure. If a die has six faces, one can only choose among six possibilities. Chance does not invent routes; it distributes probabilities within an already defined space of possibilities.

Nor does destiny decide. Destiny is not a plan, nor a purpose, nor a hidden cause. Destiny is pure inertia. It is the persistence of a global trajectory in the face of local deviations. It does not push, does not judge, does not correct. It remains. It defines the contour of the space of possibilities and restricts the accessible routes without fixing each step.

Will, on the other hand, is neither chance nor inertia. Faced with a bifurcation — a moment where the future is a fog of options — the complex system does not toss a coin. It acts as an intelligent filter: it uses objectives, needs, memory, and internal states to tip the balance toward a specific trajectory. It does not create new laws nor break the framework; it biases choices within the space that the contour allows.

Seen this way, becoming is neither a written script nor unpredictable chaos. It is a statistical problem with a contour: the framework defines what is possible, chance distributes probabilities, will biases choices, and hyperscopic inertia maintains the general course.

Free will does not consist of escaping the contour, but of traversing it without a script.¹⁰

The scope of this agency is not fixed. The captain of a cruise ship possesses a mesoscopic radius of action that covers the entire ship; their will, amplified by the helm — a mechanism that extends the causal chain of their action — allows them to choose routes within the hyperscopic contour of the ocean. A passenger, on the other hand, has a much smaller scope, limited to the corridors they can walk through. Both exercise real will, but with different scopes. Technology is, in this sense, an expansion of mesoscopic scope: it amplifies the agent's causal chain beyond their body. This scope is scalable — from the individual to entire civilizations, as illustrated by the Kardashev scale — but always asymptotic to the hyperscopic, which remains immune.

5. Essential irreversibility

That is why there is no return. To move toward the future it is necessary to pass through the present, and the present irreversibly consumes what was. Living, interacting, and deciding imply loss. The past ceases to exist because it has been used.¹¹ Irreversibility is not a defect of the world: it is its essential condition.

The result is not a tragic universe nor an arbitrary one, but a hybrid universe. In the microscopic, there is possibility. In the hyperscopic, inertia dominates. In the mesoscopic, will emerges as a cascade of actions triggered by the collapse of indeterminacy and constrained by macroscopic inertia.

Will does not exist to twist the order of the cosmos, but to write local trajectories within a stable framework.

6. Principle of ontological compatibility

The classical antinomy between freedom and determinism is a poorly posed problem. It arises from assuming that all reality operates under the same causal regime and that, therefore, freedom and determinism must coexist on the same level or exclude each other. Neither is true.

The stratified architecture of reality resolves the contradiction by locating each term in its correct stratum:

The hyperscopic operates under structural inertia: there statistical determinism dominates and individual will does not reach. The microscopic operates under indeterminacy: there there is neither will nor decision, only ontological openness. The mesoscopic is the territory of agency: complex systems that capture the openness of the lower level and operate within the contour of the upper level to produce trajectories with direction.

These three regimes do not contradict each other: they overlap. Freedom does not need to escape determinism because it lives in a stratum where hard determinism does not operate. hyperscopic determinism does not deny freedom because it does not descend to the level where will acts. Microscopic indeterminacy does not dissolve stability because its effects are averaged out at the global scale.

To understand why this architecture is not one philosophical option among others but the only one compatible with the existence of real agency, consider the only three possible scenarios:

Purely deterministic universe. If there were no ontological openness at the microscopic level, everything would be fixed from the initial conditions. Will would be a cinematic illusion: you would read this text because you were already destined to do so, not because you chose to. The future would be an already existing coordinate, and choice logically impossible.

Purely indeterminate universe. If there were no hyperscopic inertia, there would be no stable contour on which to act. Reality would dissolve into pure fluctuation: actions would have no predictable consequences, objects would not persist, and agency would lack ground on which to operate.

Three-level hybrid universe. Only this design makes simultaneously possible the openness necessary for something new to occur and the stability necessary for actions to have consequences. It is the only framework where will makes sense.¹²

The cosmos possesses an architecture that allows the emergence of beings with will, but it is not designed for them. There is no inscribed purpose, nor ontological privilege. The possibility that agents with free will exist does not imply that the universe was built for their appearance.

This principle resolves the apparent paradox of our condition: we are subjectively meaningful and, at the same time, cosmically insignificant.¹³ Our experience is central to us, but marginal to the structure of the cosmos. There is no contradiction in that.

Compatibility does not imply intention. The fact that the universe admits life, consciousness, and will does not make it their purpose.

Destiny is not a sentence. It is what prevents everything from dissolving.

Free will does not challenge the universe: it emerges within it as a consequence of its own architecture.

7. Agency, rules, and responsibility

Agency always operates within a contour. That contour is not composed solely of the physical laws of the hyperscopic level: in the human and historical domain it also includes rules, norms, and institutions that define the space of possible consequences for each trajectory.

Rules function as boundary conditions of mesoscopic scope. They do not eliminate will nor fix each step, but they establish what consequences follow which choices. In this sense they operate analogously to hyperscopic inertia: they do not dictate the decision, but they make each decision have a determinable outcome.

A direct consequence follows from this: whoever knows the rules and chooses a trajectory within that contour assumes responsibility for its consequences. Agency cannot be invoked to evade the effects of one's own choices, because precisely agency is the capacity to choose among trajectories with known consequences. Moral and legal responsibility is proportional to the agent's mesoscopic scope: the greater the radius of action on the actualization of local reality, the greater the responsibility for the outcome of that bias.

Clarification on Libet experiments
In the 1980s, Benjamin Libet measured, using electroencephalography, that brain activity associated with a simple voluntary movement — the so‑called "readiness potential" — precedes the subjective awareness of having made the decision to move by several hundred milliseconds. This finding was interpreted by some as evidence that conscious will is a retrospective illusion: the brain would have already "decided" before the subject was aware of it.

However, this interpretation confuses the map with the territory. Libet's experiments do not deny will: they measure a particular degree of reflexive awareness about a single type of simple, repetitive motor action. What they reveal is that the brain performs planning or "preloading" of actions (pipelining) before the conscious final command, analogous to speculative execution processes in modern microprocessors, which anticipate instructions before they are explicitly requested.

This pre‑loading does not eliminate agency: consciousness can act as a veto or ratification of the already initiated process, as Libet himself recognized. The neural system does not toss a coin at the moment of action; it constructs possible trajectories in advance and filters them according to objectives and internal states. That part of that processing is subconscious does not imply that the agent is not the source of the bias: the complete architecture of the system — including its non‑conscious layers — is precisely what constitutes will as an emergent property.¹⁴

Glossary of key terms

Actualization: Process by which reality transforms itself in the present, reusing the constituents of the previous configuration. It is not creation from nothing nor destruction: it is continuous and economical overwriting.
Mesoscopic scope: Radius of an agent's causal chain: the set of states of reality that its will can bias. It is not determined by the agent's physical size but by the reach of its actions. It can be amplified through technology.
Ontological openness: Property of the microscopic level by which the set of possible histories of a system is always greater than one. It is not chaos nor pure chance: it is the condition that prevents the system from being a deterministic automaton. It should not be confused with quantum indeterminacy; it is a broader ontological category.
Contour: The space of accessible trajectories for an agent, defined by hyperscopic inertia. It does not dictate each step, but restricts which routes are possible. Free will consists of traversing that contour without a pre‑established script.
Destiny: In this framework, it is not a plan nor a hidden cause. It is the structural inertia of the hyperscopic level: the statistical persistence of global trajectories that defines the contour of the possible without fixing each individual step.
Microscopic indeterminacy: Inability of any system to reproduce exactly the same process under the same conditions at the microscopic scale. It is the ingredient that makes the system non‑deterministic. It does not require anchoring in any particular physical theory.
Hyperscopic level: Stratum of reality where the great cosmological inertias operate: persistent regularities and global trajectories. It behaves deterministically not because its constituents are individually deterministic, but because the scale averages out local indeterminacies. It is immune to individual will.
Mesoscopic level: Intermediate stratum where agency emerges. Composed of systems sufficiently complex to capture microscopic openness and turn it into direction, and sufficiently local not to be absorbed by hyperscopic inertia. It is the territory of human, biological, and historical will.
Microscopic level: Deepest stratum of reality, characterized by indeterminacy. It possesses neither will nor decision: its function is ontological, not deliberative. Without it, the system would be an automaton.
Perspectival presentism: Ontological position according to which only the present exists, indexed to the reference system of each entity. The past is not archived: its constituents form the present. The future is not written: it is the open consequence of what happens now.
Intelligent bias: Capacity of a complex system to tilt the probability distribution toward a specific trajectory, using objectives, memory, and internal states. It is the central operation of will: it does not create new possibilities nor break the contour, but chooses which way to go within it.
Overwriting: See Actualization.
