Frequently Asked Questions

Answers extracted from the paper Destiny and Will (R05)

1 What does the paper Destiny and Will promise that other positions do not offer?
A third option between "free will is an illusion" and "free will is a miracle": freedom is real, but not what we usually think.
2 Why has the problem of free will remained open for 2,500 years?
Because it simultaneously touches time, causality, matter, and consciousness. It is a fundamental problem in the most literal sense.
3 What is the central thesis of the paper?
Time is a virtual dimension of transformation; reality has three levels (microscopic, hyperscopic, mesoscopic); will emerges at the mesoscopic level as the capacity to orient possibilities; free will consists of traversing a contour without a script, in a universe devoid of teleological purpose.
4 Does free will challenge the universe?
No. It emerges within it as a consequence of its own architecture.
5 Why is time not a container of events?
Because if it were a container like space, past, present, and future would exist simultaneously, violating all conservation principles. As a virtual dimension measuring transformation, it allows the same mathematical model without violating mass-energy conservation.
6 What does "only the present exists ontologically" mean?
That the past is not stored and cannot be retrieved, and the future is not written. Reality updates itself by overwriting the previous configuration, relocating its components.
7 What happens to the past according to this framework?
It is consumed to build the present. Its constituents (matter, energy) persist, but the previous configuration disappears.
8 Does the universe keep backups of the past?
No. To exist is to continuously overwrite reality.
9 What analogy is used to explain overwriting?
A chess game: pieces are not destroyed; the previous configuration ceases to exist because some pieces changed position or were captured.
10 What is the difference between "perspectival presentism" and "absolute simultaneity"?
In perspectival presentism, the now is local and indexed to the system, depending on the observer's relative perspective; in absolute simultaneity, time would be universal so all observers would see the same thing.
11 Does the future already exist as something given?
No. It is the open consequence of what happens now, not a fixed coordinate. Otherwise, this discussion would make no sense.
12 What is the block universe or eternalism?
The idea that past, present, and future exist simultaneously as coordinates in a 4D block. The "now" has no special status.
13 Why does the block universe make free will impossible?
If the future already exists as a coordinate, your "decision" tomorrow has already happened. There is nothing to decide or do.
14 What is "overwriting"?
A metaphor for the process by which the universe updates the present: it transforms with the minimum necessary change, reusing as many elements as possible from the previous configuration.
15 Why is irreversibility a necessary condition for the emergence of will?
If the past were recoverable and the future a pre‑existing coordinate, there would be no room for something genuinely new.
16 Why is there no return in time?
Because the present irreversibly consumes what was.
17 Is irreversibility a defect of the world?
No, it is its essential condition, necessary for will.
18 What are the three levels of reality?
Microscopic (indeterminacy), hyperscopic (structural inertia), and mesoscopic (territory of will, superimposed on the previous two).
19 What is the mistake of believing in a single causal regime?
Assuming that all reality obeys the same kind of necessity (deterministic or indeterministic), so that freedom and determinism must exclude each other.
20 What is the function of the microscopic level?
It provides basal indeterminacy or ontological openness. It prevents the universe from being a deterministic automaton.
21 Is there will at the microscopic level?
No. There is neither will nor decision, only basic ontological indeterminacy.
22 What does the hyperscopic level do?
It defines the contour of the possible through structural inertia (gravity, conservation laws, thermodynamics). It is destiny as stable ground, not as a script.
23 Why does the hyperscopic level behave deterministically?
Not because its constituents are individually deterministic, but because the scale averages out local indeterminacies, making them negligible.
24 Can will influence the hyperscopic level?
No. The scale renders it immune, not due to lack of free will, but because of the limited range of actions that result from will.
25 What does the mesoscopic level do?
It is the territory of agency. Complex systems capture microscopic openness and amplify it so that the agent is not deterministic and chooses direction through a combination of intelligent biases based on perceived current states, memories, and amplification of microscopic errors.
26 Where does will operate?
At the mesoscopic level: the human, biological, and historical realm.
27 What does will do at the mesoscopic level?
It tips the balance: decides which way to go within a domain it did not choose. It does not create new possibilities nor break the framework.
28 Is will a random collapse of quantum indeterminacy?
No. Microscopic indeterminacy makes the system non‑deterministic, but it does not direct it. Direction comes from complex architecture.
29 What analogy is used to explain will as an emergent property?
A language model: it does not always repeat the same response to the same input, without being unpredictable or chaotic.
30 What does this conception of will eliminate?
The need for a "homunculus" (a little driver inside the head).
31 What three possible universe scenarios does the paper propose?
(1) Purely deterministic: freedom is illusory. (2) Purely indeterminate: without inertia, actions have no durable effects. (3) The three‑level hybrid model: the only one compatible with real agency.
32 Is chance the same as chaos?
No. Chance distributes probabilities within an already defined space of possibilities.
33 What is destiny according to the paper?
It is not a plan nor a hidden cause. It is pure inertia: the persistence of a global trajectory that defines the contour of the possible.
34 Does destiny push or correct?
No. It simply remains. It defines the contour without fixing each step.
35 What does will do at a bifurcation?
It does not toss a coin. It acts as an intelligent filter using goals, memory, and internal states.
36 What does free will consist of?
Not in escaping the contour, but in traversing it without a script.
37 Is the universe a written script or unpredictable chaos?
It is a statistical problem with a contour: the framework defines what is possible, chance distributes probabilities, will biases choices, and hyperscopic inertia maintains the general course.
38 What is will not?
It is not chance (a random decision is not free) nor disguised determinism (acting from desires determined by external causes). It is not a homunculus either.
39 What four factors does intelligent bias integrate?
Goals, memory, internal state, and causal models.
40 What does chance do in the process (together with destiny and will)?
It distributes probabilities over possible futures at the microscopic level without affecting the hyperscopic level.
41 What does destiny do in the process?
It defines the contour (set of possible futures) through conservation laws, gravity, thermodynamics, and deterministic trajectories.
42 What does will do in the process?
It biases the probability distribution: it modifies the relative probability of the possibilities that the contour allows.
43 How does the paper define free will?
"Capacity to traverse a contour without a script." Genuinely biasing the evolution of the present within the boundaries of destiny.
44 Is the scope of will fixed for everyone?
No. The captain of a cruise ship has a greater scope than a passenger.
45 What role does technology play?
Expansion of mesoscopic scope: it amplifies the agent's causal chain beyond their body.
46 Can mesoscopic scope reach the hyperscopic?
No, it is always asymptotic to the hyperscopic, which remains immune.
47 Does technology expand free will?
Yes, because it amplifies mesoscopic scope. More scope, more freedom and more responsibility.
48 Will and biography?
Will as intelligent bias turns a physical trajectory into a biography: narrative coherence.
49 What function do social rules serve?
They are boundary conditions of mesoscopic scope: they establish the consequences of each choice.
50 Who is responsible for the consequences?
Whoever knows the rules and chooses within the contour assumes responsibility.
51 Is moral responsibility equal for everyone?
No. It is proportional to the agent's mesoscopic scope.
52 How is proportional responsibility applied in ethics?
Responsibility is proportional to mesoscopic scope (power).
53 What did Libet's experiment measure?
That brain activity (readiness potential) precedes subjective awareness of the decision.
54 Does that deny will?
No. It reveals a preloading of actions (pipelining). Consciousness can act as veto or ratification.
55 What role does consciousness play according to Libet in this framework?
It can veto or ratify the initiated process. Subconscious processing does not imply that the agent is not the source of the bias.
56 What is will in relation to the brain?
The complete architecture of the system (conscious and non‑conscious layers) constitutes will as an emergent property.
57 Why is the freedom‑determinism antinomy ill‑posed?
Because it assumes a single causal regime. Reality is stratified.
58 How is the contradiction resolved?
By locating each term in its stratum: hyperscopic (inertia), microscopic (indeterminacy), mesoscopic (agency).
59 What would happen in a purely deterministic universe?
Everything fixed from initial conditions. Will would be an illusion.
60 What would happen in a purely indeterminate universe?
Without a stable contour, actions would have no predictable consequences. Agency would lack ground.
61 What is the principle of ontological compatibility?
Determinism, indeterminacy, and agency coexist in different strata of the same reality without canceling each other out.
62 Is the universe designed for will?
No. Its architecture allows it, but it was not built for it. No purpose is inscribed.
63 Why does architecture not imply design?
Anti‑finalism: architecture admits will as oxygen admits life, but the atmosphere was not designed for that.
64 Is there a contradiction between being subjectively meaningful and cosmically insignificant?
No. Our experience is central to us, marginal to the structure of the cosmos.
65 Is destiny a sentence?
No. It is what prevents everything from dissolving into chaos.
66 In what sense are we subjectively meaningful?
Our decisions have real effects on local reality.
67 In what sense are we cosmically insignificant?
hyperscopic inertia does not change in response to our choices. The Sun will still rise.
68 What is anti‑finalism as liberation?
The universe without an inscribed purpose is not nihilistic: we construct purpose within the contour.
69 If the microscopic level has indeterminacy, that only introduces chance, not freedom. How does the paper prevent will from being just disguised chance?
Will is not chance because intelligent bias is not an equiprobable distribution. The complex system uses goals and memory to *tip* probabilities, not to toss a coin. Chance without bias would not be free; bias without openness would be determinism. The paper combines both.
70 If will emerges from a physical system, then it is causally reducible to the physical. Isn't that disguised epiphenomenalism?
The paper rejects hard reductionism: will is a property of the mesoscopic level with real causal efficacy. It is not an epiphenomenon because intelligent bias modifies the evolution of the system at its own level, without breaking physical laws.
71 Either the universe is deterministic (no freedom) or indeterministic (decision is random). The paper does not offer a third way, only a mixture. Where is genuine "freedom"?
The third way is intelligent bias on an indeterminate basis but with contour restrictions. Freedom is not "avoiding causality", but *orienting* the evolution of the present through an internal criterion. The paper defines freedom as "traversing a contour without a script", not as the absence of all restriction.
72 Free will only requires acting according to our desires without external coercion. Why complicate with three levels and ontological openness?
Classical compatibilism does not solve the problem of hard determinism: if everything is written, "acting according to your desires" is also written. The paper offers an ontological basis (microscopic openness) so that desire is not an illusion. It does not deny compatibilism; it grounds it in a realistic metaphysics of time.
73 The paper speaks of "contour" and "scope", but that limits the absolute freedom to create values. Isn't that a return to soft determinism?
The paper accepts the finitude of freedom: no matter how much one wishes, one cannot fly against gravity. But within the contour, freedom is real and non‑teleological. Anti‑finalism is compatible with existentialism: purpose is not given, it is built. The difference is that the paper adds a physical basis to Sartre's "condemned to be free".
74 Will is not a calculable "intelligent bias"; it is corporeal, perceptual, and pre‑reflective. Doesn't the paper reduce will to an algorithm?
The paper explicitly rejects the homunculus and decision as a conscious algorithm. "Intelligent bias" includes non‑conscious layers, implicit memory, and bodily states.
75 Freedom must be noumenal, not phenomenal. If will is in the physical world (mesoscopic), it is determined by empirical conditions and not transcendental.
The paper rejects dualism of worlds. Freedom is a real property of the phenomenal world at its mesoscopic level. It does not need a noumenal realm. Compatibility is achieved by stratification, not by flight from the phenomenon.
76 Destiny as hyperscopic inertia is simply Deus sive Natura. Will is an illusion of finite modes. The paper does not show how intelligent bias escapes geometric necessity.
The paper accepts hyperscopic inertia as necessary at large scales, but microscopic openness introduces a real contingency that Spinoza did not admit. Will does not "escape" necessity; it operates in the interstices of the indeterminate. It is not an illusion, but a real level of organization.
77 General relativity shows that time is a coordinate inseparable from space. "Perspectival presentism" seems incompatible with relativity, where the "now" depends on the observer.
The paper knows this and uses the term "perspectival presentism" precisely to incorporate relativity: the present is not global, but indexed to each reference system. There is no conflict: the present is local and real for each observer. What is denied is the block universe as ontological reality.
78 Quantum indeterminacy is not "ontological openness" in the sense of free will; it is objective probability. Isn't the paper projecting human properties onto physics?
The paper does not identify ontological openness with quantum indeterminacy; it is a broader category. Quantum mechanics is a *case* of it, but not the ultimate foundation. Openness means that the set of possible histories > 1. No will is projected onto the quantum.
79 If the hyperscopic level averages out indeterminacies, then at the mesoscopic scale statistical determinism is overwhelming. Where is the room for intelligent bias if fluctuations are tiny?
The paper does not say that the mesoscopic level is dominated by fluctuations. It says that complex systems can *amplify* small differences (butterfly effect). Intelligent bias does not require large fluctuations, but sensitive boundary conditions.
80 Macroscopic irreversibility comes from low‑entropy initial conditions, not from an ontological "overwriting". How does the paper respond to the cosmological origin of the arrow?
The paper does not contradict that. Overwriting is an ontological description of the transformation process, not an alternative to thermodynamics. The fact that entropy increases is precisely what makes overwriting irreversible. There is no incompatibility.
81 If only the present exists, how do we explain the experience of duration and memory? Memory seems to imply a past that somehow persists.
Memory does not require the past to exist as a place. Memory is a *present configuration* of the brain that records previous transformations. It is analogous to marks on a tree: the mark is not the past, it is a present trace. The paper distinguishes between "existence of the past" and "effects of the past in the present".
82 At the Planck scale, time may be discrete or non‑existent. Is perspectival presentism compatible with loop quantum gravity or causal sets?
The paper is agnostic about quantum gravity, but its framework is compatible: if time is discrete, each "present" would be an update from one state to another. Overwriting remains the operation. Continuity is not required.
83 If the universe constantly overwrites itself, how do we explain the cosmic microwave background? It is a "fossil" from the distant past that seems to exist now as a record.
The background radiation is not the "stored" past; it is a present configuration of photons that traveled from recombination. That journey is part of the overwriting process. There is no "layer" of the past; there is a chain of transformations that today manifests as that spectrum.
84 "Intelligent bias" requires a model of the world to predict consequences. But that model is in the brain. Isn't that a regression: to decide I need a model, to have a model I need to learn, and learning requires previous decisions?
The paper does not demand an explicit and complete model. Bias can be based on heuristics, emotions, and implicit memory. Learning is co‑evolutionary: there is no infinite regress because the system develops in real time, updating its models while deciding. It is a coupled process.
85 If the microscopic level is indeterminate, that implies the future is not written. But then will cannot "bias" probabilities without violating quantum unitarity (evolution is deterministic between measurements).
The paper does not commit to a particular interpretation of quantum mechanics. "Ontological openness" may occur in collapse. The point is that, whatever the physics, the mesoscopic system introduces a bias in the effective distribution. It does not violate unitarity if it concerns effective initial conditions.
86 "Will" and "free will" are concepts from folk psychology. The paper reifies them without showing their specific neural correlate. Isn't that just a narrative?
The paper proposes that will is an emergent property of complex systems with memory, goals, and causal models. That has a neural correlate: recurrent neural networks with plasticity and reward functions. It is not a magical reification, but a hypothesis about how certain architectures produce bias.
87 Libet's experiments have been questioned and replicated with nuances. The paper uses them as support, but current literature shows that "preparation" is not a decision.
The paper does not depend on the empirical validity of Libet. It uses it as a didactic example that consciousness is not the initiator. Even if Libet were incorrect in details, the general point (that much processing is subconscious) is solid. The framework does not collapse without Libet.
88 If will emerges from the brain, then the decision is a brain state. Isn't that deterministic if the brain obeys physical laws?
The brain operates at the mesoscopic level, which includes microscopic openness. The brain is not a deterministic automaton because its constituents have indeterminacy. The "decision" is the result of intelligent bias operating on some effects of that indeterminacy. It is neither deterministic nor random: it is goal‑oriented.
89 "Intelligent bias" seems just a description of what reinforcement learning algorithms do. Where does the conscious experience of effort and deliberation fit?
Consciousness is part of the system, but not the only mechanism. Subjective effort may be a signal from the system about the cost of biasing the distribution. The paper does not reduce experience; it assigns it a role: conscious ratification (veto) is one of the functions of the mesoscopic level.
90 If responsibility is proportional to mesoscopic scope, doesn't that justify treating the powerful as more responsible (and thus more punishable) and the oppressed as less responsible (and thus paternalism)?
The paper does not prescribe policies, but the proportionality principle is bidirectional: greater power implies greater responsibility (leaders must be accountable); lesser power implies lesser criminal responsibility but not annulment of agency. It is contrary to paternalism because it recognizes that even with reduced scope there is real will, not total incapacity.
91 "Contour" and "hyperscopic inertia" sound like an excuse for social determinism. If destiny is inertia, doesn't that legitimize the status quo?
The paper distinguishes between physical inertia (hyperscopic) and social rules. Social rules are modifiable by collective will (mesoscopic scope amplified technologically and politically). hyperscopic inertia (gravity, thermodynamics) is not social. The paper does not legitimize the status quo; on the contrary, it shows that rules are contingent boundary conditions.
92 If the microscopic level has indeterminacy, that introduces a random element into behavior. How can criminal law be based on responsibility if actions are not fully controllable?
Criminal law does not require absolute control, but the capacity to bias behavior through norms. Indeterminacy does not excuse because will (bias) is what the law evaluates. An action can be unpredictable in detail but intention‑guided. Punishment is not for deterministic certainty, but for the bias to discern consequences.
93 The framework adds nothing useful for practical decision‑making. It is unnecessary metaphysics. What is it for?
It serves to dissolve false antinomies that paralyze practical thinking (e.g., "if the brain is physical, there is no freedom"). It provides an ontological justification for agency that allows taking responsibility seriously without falling into dualism or hard determinism. It is useful for philosophy of law, neuroethics, and AI.
94 An AI system with goals and memory can bias probabilities. Does that mean it has will according to the paper? Where is the line?
The paper defines will as intelligent bias in mesoscopic systems. An AI could qualify if it has: (a) openness in its own dynamics (non‑deterministic), (b) internal goals and memory, (c) causal models, and (d) the ability to influence the world. There is no magic line; it is a matter of degree and architecture. The paper does not exclude strong AI in principle.
95 Human will has qualia (feeling of effort, decision). An AI without consciousness would not have "real will" even if it biases probabilities.
The paper does not make consciousness the criterion of will. It defines will by its function (intelligent bias), not by phenomenal experience. This is deliberate to avoid the mystery of dualism. If one prefers to reserve "will" for conscious systems, the paper offers a broader notion of "agency".
96 If an AI has will (intelligent bias), then it is not controllable. Isn't that dangerous?
The paper does not imply that will is uncontrollable. The hyperscopic contour and rules also apply to AI. Will operates within constraints. The danger does not come from agency per se, but from misaligned goals. The framework helps to think in terms of contours and scopes.
97 Can the paper offer an empirical criterion to distinguish a system with real will from one that merely simulates bias?
The criterion is not purely behaviorist, but structural: the system must have ontological openness at its constituent level (not be a deterministic automaton), and its bias must be internally generated (not just a response to fixed input). In practice, one could test with bifurcation situations where the output is neither a deterministic function of the input nor purely random, but shows consistency with internal goals over time. "If the simulation is close enough to reality, it might be indistinguishable."
98 If a system simulates consciousness so realistically that no test can tell it apart from a conscious being, how can we know it is not real consciousness?
We cannot know with certainty. If a simulation is functionally identical, all behavioral and neurological tests would yield the same results. In that case, the difference between "real consciousness" and "perfect simulation of consciousness" becomes metaphysical, not empirical. The paper does not solve this problem (the "other hard problem" of consciousness). However, it provides a structural criterion: the presence of ontological openness (genuine indeterminacy not reproducible by a deterministic automaton) could serve as a watermark, although in practice an exact quantum simulation would be indistinguishable. Ultimately, the distinction may be undecidable, but this does not invalidate the usefulness of the concept of will for agency and responsibility.
